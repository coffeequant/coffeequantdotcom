Risk Neutral Probability

 
 


0.5 * 1 - 0.5 * 1 = 0 (in technical terms this is the expected value at any instant)

Let’s say I rigged the game so that coin toss is somehow manipulated so that probability of heads coming up is 33% and tails coming up is 67%. If I propose the same game like before then it’s not fair. Why?


0.33 * 1 - 0.67 * 1 = _NOT EQUAL_ to zero

What about this?

0.33 * (0.5/0.33) + 0.67*(0.5/0.67) * -1 = 0

The numbers in brackets force the probabilities to 0.5 to make the game fair. Of course it’s just symbol manipulation but that’s what it is!


I can also say p = 0.33 and 1-p =(approx) 0.67

so p*1+(1-p)(-1) = _NOT EQUAL_ to zero

1*p*(0.5/p) + (1-p) *(0.5/(1-p))*-1 = 0. These numbers 0.5/p and 0.5/(1-p) are basically loading the probabilities to make them fair.


In real world the coin might be loaded with 33% /67% probability but in some fair game I change these probabilities. These probabilities which make the cash flow at the end fair to everyone are called risk neutral probabilities.


If this is clear I can actually price a crude derivative with this concept, without any complicated formula. It’s about to expire in let’s say a minute or so.

Derivative pricing is all about cash flows at maturity or at expiry. To generate these cash flows you have stocks or a single stock (for a single derivative) which you buy or sell in some quantity to replicate it.

Stock price currently is at 100 and probability of stock going to 101 is 60% and stock going to 99 is 40%. No other scenario is possible and I want to price a 100 strike call.


So for case 1) It reaches 101 - Cash flow is 1

case 2) It reaches 99 - cash flow is 0


Price = 0.5*1+0.5*0 = 0.5. Why 0.5? Coz if I had bought 0.5 quantity of stock at the beginning of 1 minute and sold the call option my portfolio value is


Case 1) 1 - 0.5*(101-100) = 0.5

Case 2) 0 - 0.5*(99-100) = 0.5


which is same as option value...and well this is where you have the concept of replicating portfolio. But what about 60% and 40%? Why didn’t I use them. Well they are real world probabilities and I don’t care about them. I forced them to be 50,50 coz otherwise I can’t make this game fair. Yes derivatives trading is a gamble or a game. above example is an extremely simple, but of course by definition


Risk Neutral Probability - A probability which eliminates arbitrage when pricing the derivative. Or make the game a fair value game.
