Traders and Quants have both loved Poker, and as usual Quants have beaten the traders numerous times! Almost everyone knows Hold’Em poker rules. Calculating probabilities and expectations is easy once you get the hang of it.


Example: If I wager 10Bucks on a hand, and I got 2 K’s. Three cards on the table are 2Q’s + 1A. There are total of 4 players playing, so out of total 52 cards 11 are in play and 41 left. These 41 will decide the two left over cards and rest 11 will decide weather I continue to play. To simplify let’s leave the 2 cards to be opened out of the calculation. My hand is simply KK + QQ + A, and it can be beaten by


  1. 1. Q + (Any card)

  2. 2. A + any card

so simple probability calculation results in

  1. 1.(2/47) * 3 (3 = number of players)

  2. 2. (3/47) * 3


This is a whooping  31% probability of loss, so 68% probability of winning. Should I go all in. If the pot till now is 1000 and I have wagered 300, then expected value calculation comes out to be 585, so even if I increase my wager till 2000, it’s all good, as expected value is still positive. [1000 * 68% - 2100 * 31%]. Of course since the other two cards can mess up the scenario I would choose a lesser amount.

The problem with this approach is I don’t know how much of my total amount to bet. If I have total of 10,000 bucks I would like to control the fraction of my total wealth I am betting and increase or decrease it as per my wins and loses. If I start with 100 for first hand then total amount becomes


100 x [1 + fraction x outcome]

After 2 hands my amount becomes

100 x [ 1 + fraction x outcome1] x [ 1 + fraction x outcome2]

and so on for a total of N hands, I play all night!

Since in poker outcome of each hand is independent I can write expected value of growth rate as

(1/N) Log (100 x Product of(1 + fraction x outcome(i))))

Log of product becomes a simple summation and ignoring Log(100)/N, we have the growth rate as

E[Log(1+fraction x outcome(i)]

The taylor series expansion will expand it as

E[fraction x outcome(i) - 1/2 x fraction^2 * outcome(i)^2 + higher order terms]

maximizing it is simple differentiation which gives fraction = Mean / sigma^2. Here sigma is number of standard deviations away from mean value.
