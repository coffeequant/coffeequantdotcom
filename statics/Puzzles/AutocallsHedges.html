Autocallable - 2

 

A structured product market has a basic flaw that you can’t copyright any product. If you issue an exotic another bank might easily replicate that product and if the product is popular it will be replicated by number of banks. This is of course good for the investor as it results in highly competitive pricing but for a trader it sometimes becomes a nightmare.


Let us consider the hypothetical example of an autocallable issued today with autocall dates at 9-Sept-2012, 9-Jan-2013, 9-May-2013, 9-Sept-2013, 9-Jan-2014. Other banks might have priced a similar product with autocall dates close to september month. This is not a vague assumption as there is a huge market of autocallables and a number of them have close autocall dates. Take an emerging market - let’s assume NIFTY Index (nseindia.com) where daily volume for a particular strike is around 5 - 10 million contracts.

Bucketing


Vega bucketing in general splits the greeks into particular maturity bucket. For example you can calculate 1m vega risk by bumping the 1m maturity volatility for all strikes and calculating the vega. All other maturities are kept constant. As discussed in previous post, the trader is long vega above the barrier, so the hedges are short vega. As the underlying rallies - let’s say 15 days before the autocall date.


  1. 1.Probability of autocall increases

  2. a)Shorter maturity requires rebalancing

  3. b)Longer dated vega hedges need to be bought back, and short dated vegas need to be sold


Due to a) and b) - The demand for upside calls increases

This is interesting if the total notional of autocalls in the market is 1000 million,

So hedging would require a total of 12.75 million contracts to be executed in a day, and the same number of short dated contracts for completing the rebalance. This is twice the size of daily Nifty volume.


This buying as a whole will cause some directional affect on the index. For example in this case the put spreads need to be bought back and rolled to shorter maturity put spreads. Long dated put spreads buying might not affect the market that much, but shorting “short dated” put spreads might push the market further down. As explained there will be a huge demand for lower OTM strike puts. Sellers of those puts will hedge by shorting futures and hence push the market downward.


  1. 2.Market rallies downwards

  2. a)Probability of knock in put being triggered increases

  3. b)Traders will want to sell downside puts in advance to hedge


The one’s who bought the puts will hedge the delta by buying stock, hence pushing the market upward. Combined affect can lead to a crash, coz you have multiple participants hedging exactly the same thing.
