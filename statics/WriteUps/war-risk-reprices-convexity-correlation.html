War Risk Reprices the Curve: Oil Convexity, Gas Shock, and the

Macro Oil Convexity Correlation

The tape: one headline, three prices

War headlines hit and the market instantly asks three questions:

1) How big is the oil tail?
2) Is gas a second-order bomb?
3) Do correlations stay “local”… or go “global”?

Over the weekend, crude didn’t tiptoe. It jumped: Brent and WTI posted
roughly ~9% intraday gains after the US and Israel strikes on Iran were
reported. That’s not a “view.” That’s the market buying tail insurance in
real time.

Why this is convexity (in one sentence)

Oil doesn’t reprice like a stock. In war regimes, probabilities change
discontinuously, so prices gap instead of trending.

Convexity, in trader-English

If Brent is ~78, the distribution is not “77–79 with some noise.”

It becomes:


  • Base case: shipping slows, premiums widen, oil drifts higher.

  • Bad case: chokepoint risk (Hormuz) turns into a logistics problem.

  • Ugly case: insurance + routing + availability all jump together.

That’s why VLCC rates are the real tell. One widely-circulated print:
supertanker earnings to haul ME crude to China surged to ~$424k/day at
the peak. Even if you haircut the number, the direction is the point: war
risk monetizes through freight + insurance before it fully shows up in
spot.

Gas: the “quiet” convexity

If oil is the headline, LNG is the fragility.

European gas closed ~39% higher after reports that Qatar suspended LNG
output (exactly the kind of “single point of failure” the market hates).

That’s an important convexity lesson:

  • Oil can be buffered by spare capacity and inventory narratives.
  • LNG disruptions can feel binary because infrastructure and routing are
less flexible.

Correlation creep: first dispersion, then “everything sells”

Early in geopolitical shocks you often get dispersion:


  • energy up

  • airlines down

  • defense up

  • consumer discretionary wobbles

Index looks “fine,” but single names are violent.

Later, if stress becomes systemic, correlation tightens. The same story
repeats:


  • first: “this is sector-specific”

  • then: “this is funding-specific”

  • finally: “sell what you can”

That’s why the best war trade isn’t just direction. It’s owning
convexity where it’s underpaid, while keeping a plan for correlation
going to 1.

The usual trap

People buy index puts after the war premium is already in the screen.
You pay peak vol… for protection you needed earlier.

A clean positioning checklist (no heroics)

1) If you must be long energy: prefer structures that survive chop
Example idea (conceptual): call spreads or call + put financed collars, so
you don’t donate premium if the headline fades.

2) Watch freight / gas as “regime indicators”
If freight and gas keep screaming, the oil tail is not done.

3) Assume correlation can snap higher
Any book that is “cute dispersion” without a correlation plan is one Friday
away from pain.

CQ punchline

War regimes do one thing consistently:

They turn “volatility” into convexity
and turn “diversification” into a timing problem.

Trade the tails with a plan, not with vibes.
