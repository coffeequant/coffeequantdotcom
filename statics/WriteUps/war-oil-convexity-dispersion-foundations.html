War headlines and oil options: why convexity matters

The US and Israel have begun striking targets across Iran. Iran has
retaliated. Airspace closures across the Gulf. Missile exchanges.
Regional escalation risk.

Brent had already rallied nearly ~20% year-to-date on tension
buildup and touched ~$73/bbl before markets closed.

When headlines look like this, markets don’t think in straight lines.

They think in tails.
And tails are convexity.


Convexity in oil, explained like a trader

Convexity is simple:

Small moves do little.
Large moves change everything.

Peace regime (typical oil behavior):


  • Supply steady

  • Shipping lanes open

  • Spare capacity “known”

  • Volatility moderate

War regime (oil behavior changes):


  • Strait of Hormuz risk

  • Infrastructure damage risk

  • Sanctions tighten

  • Insurance and freight spike

  • Retaliation timing uncertain

Oil in war doesn’t “trend.”
It jumps.

Example intuition (not a forecast, just shape):

If oil is $70:


  • Calm scenario: $65–$75

  • Escalation scenario: $90–$100+

Downside is usually more gradual.
Upside can gap.

That asymmetry is convexity.

Why “just buying oil” is not risk management

Most investors respond to war by:


  • Buying oil futures

  • Buying energy stocks

  • Buying oil calls

That can work. But it concentrates risk in one direction.

If OPEC+ increases supply.
If escalation fades.
If diplomacy surprises.

Oil can drop sharply and fast.

Directional long-oil is a bet.
War is not directional.
War is distributional.

That’s why the better framework is: dispersion + correlation regimes.


Dispersion: the adult version of “trading war”

Dispersion is the idea that instead of betting on “the index,” you bet
on differences between components.

In conflict regimes:


  • Oil producers may spike

  • Airlines may sell off

  • Defense may rally

  • Industrials may split (winners vs losers)

  • Gold may surge

  • Broad indices can look “sleepy” while single names whip around

That widening gap between winners and losers is dispersion.

A common pattern:


  • Dispersion widens first

  • Correlation rises later

A simple dispersion example (no formulas)

Imagine:


  • Oil at $70

  • Energy stocks up +5%

  • Airlines down -6%

  • Index down -1%

A dispersion-style mindset:


  • Own convexity where the shock hits first (energy)

  • Own protection where the shock hurts (airlines)

  • Neutralize broad index exposure (so you’re not “betting the market”)

If war escalates:


  • Energy can accelerate

  • Airlines can deteriorate

  • The gap widens (dispersion)

If war fades:


  • Both can revert

  • Your loss is structured, not existential

You didn’t bet on a market crash.
You traded spread widening.

That’s dispersion.


The correlation trap (and why professionals care)

Early in conflict:


  • Dispersion widens

  • Correlation stays moderate

If conflict becomes systemic:


  • Correlation spikes

  • Everything sells

  • Diversification collapses

This is why you never ignore regime shifts.

Good dispersion structures don’t pretend correlation can’t spike.
They are built so that:


  • you can make money when spreads widen,

  • and you don’t get instantly destroyed if correlation goes to 1.

(That’s the real point of “convex dispersion.”)


Oil convexity is not just price: it’s plumbing

War affects:


  • Shipping lanes (Hormuz risk)

  • Insurance premia and freight

  • Refining margins

  • OPEC+ supply decisions

  • Strategic reserves policy

So oil “volatility” is not just direction.

It’s uncertainty about the distribution.
Convexity is the market’s way of pricing the unknown tail.


Positioning checklist (intuitive)

Instead of:
“I think oil goes to 90.”

Think:
“If escalation probability rises from 10% to 25%, what breaks first?
Who benefits first?”

Instead of:
“I’ll buy the index.”

Think:
“Which assets are directly exposed winners and losers, and how do I
avoid paying peak index vol?”

War trading is about:
1) Relative performance
2) Convex asymmetry
3) Managing correlation regime risk


Clean takeaway (bookmark this)

War is not linear. It’s a probability shock.

Convexity is how markets price probability jumps.
Dispersion is how traders monetize uneven impact.

If escalation spreads: convex dispersion can win.
If escalation fades: losses are budgeted, not catastrophic.

Convexity protects. Dispersion refines.

That’s why professionals don’t “play war.”
They structure it.
