War, Oil and Convexity: Why Dispersion Is the Smart Way to

Foundations Oil Convexity Dispersion

When war hits the tape, convexity wakes up

The US and Israel have begun striking targets across Iran. Iran has
retaliated. Airspace closures across the Gulf. Missile exchanges.
Regional escalation risk.

Brent had already rallied nearly 20% year-to-date on tension buildup
and touched $73 per barrel before markets closed.

When headlines look like this, traders don’t think in straight lines.

They think in convexity.


What convexity really means in oil

Convexity is simple:

Small moves do little.
Large moves change everything.

Oil pricing during peace:


  • Supply steady.

  • Shipping lanes open.

  • Spare capacity known.

  • Volatility moderate.

Oil pricing during war:


  • Strait of Hormuz risk.

  • Infrastructure damage.

  • Sanctions tighten.

  • Insurance costs spike.

  • Retaliation uncertainty.

Oil doesn’t move linearly in war.
It jumps.

That jump risk is convex.

Example intuition:

If oil is $70:


  • Peace scenario → maybe $65–75 range.

  • Escalation scenario → $90, $100, maybe higher.

The downside is gradual.
The upside can be explosive.

That asymmetry is convexity.


Why owning “just oil” is not enough

Many investors respond to war by:


  • Buying oil futures.

  • Buying energy stocks.

  • Buying oil calls.

That works — but it concentrates risk in one direction.

Here’s the problem:

If OPEC+ increases supply.
If escalation fades.
If diplomacy surprises.

Oil can drop sharply and fast.

Pure long-oil positioning is not risk management.
It is a directional bet.

War is not directional.
War is distributional.

And that’s where dispersion comes in.


Dispersion: the adult way to trade war

Dispersion is the idea that:

Instead of betting on “the index”,
you bet on differences between components.

In war environments:

  • Oil producers may spike.
  • Airlines may fall.
  • Defense stocks may rally.
  • Tech may wobble.
  • Gold may surge.
  • Broad indices may move less than individual names.

That widening gap between winners and losers is dispersion.

Convexity often appears first in single assets.
Correlation rises later.


Simple example (no formulas)

Imagine this setup:

Oil at $70.
Energy stocks up 5%.
Airlines down 6%.
Index down 1%.

If you:


  • Own energy calls

  • Own airline puts

  • Neutralize broad index exposure

You are trading dispersion.

If war escalates:


  • Energy accelerates.

  • Airlines deteriorate further.

  • Your convexity increases.

If war fades:


  • Both revert.

  • Your risk is limited by structure.

You didn’t bet on “market crash”.
You bet on spread widening.

That’s dispersion.


Why dispersion is perfect for war regimes

War produces:

  1. Sector divergence.
  2. Commodity spikes.
  3. Cross-asset decoupling.
  4. Uncertain duration.

Dispersion thrives on uncertainty in relative outcomes.

Index options are expensive in war.
Single-name convexity is often mispriced early.

By positioning in relative moves, you:

  • Reduce pure directional exposure.
  • Avoid paying peak index vol.
  • Capture convex outcomes where they emerge first.

The correlation trap

Here’s the danger:

Early in conflict:


  • Dispersion widens.

  • Correlation stays moderate.

If conflict escalates into systemic shock:


  • Correlation spikes.

  • Everything sells.

  • Diversification collapses.

This is why convexity matters.

Convex dispersion structures are powerful because:

They make money when spreads widen.
They don’t collapse immediately if correlation spikes.
They avoid the “all in one trade” trap.


Oil convexity is not just price — it’s structure

War affects:

  • Shipping lanes (Hormuz risk).
  • Insurance premia.
  • Refining margins.
  • OPEC+ supply decisions.
  • Strategic reserves policy.

Oil volatility is not just direction.
It is distribution uncertainty.

Convexity reflects the unknown tail.


How to think about positioning (intuitively)

Instead of:

“I think oil goes to 90.”

Think:

“If escalation probability rises from 10% to 25%, how do spreads behave?”

Instead of:

“I’ll buy the index.”

Think:

“Which assets benefit directly? Which suffer directly?”

War trading is about:

  1. Relative performance.
  2. Convex asymmetry.
  3. Managing correlation risk.

The elegant structure mindset

Good war positioning:

  • Own convexity in exposed sectors.
  • Avoid paying peak volatility in the index.
  • Pair winners vs losers.
  • Budget downside clearly.

Bad war positioning:

  • One big directional oil bet.
  • No hedge.
  • No awareness of correlation regime shifts.

The clean takeaway

War is not a linear event.
It is a probability shock.

Convexity is how markets price probability jumps.
Dispersion is how traders monetize uneven impact.

If escalation spreads:
Convex dispersion wins.

If escalation fades:
Losses are structured, not catastrophic.

Convexity protects.
Dispersion refines.

That is why professionals don’t “play war”.
They structure it.
