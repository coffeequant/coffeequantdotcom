Dispersion Trading 101: Correlation, Variance, and the “Index vs Components” Game

[[pill:Foundations]] [[pill:Dispersion]] [[pill:Correlation]] [[pill:Volatility]]

Think of a market index as a choir. Each stock is a singer. Index volatility is what you hear from the hallway. Single-name volatility is what you hear when you put your ear to each door.

The one-line definition

Dispersion is the wedge between: (1) how wild the singers are individually and (2) how wild the choir sounds together.

That wedge is driven largely by correlation.

Step 1: Start with two assets (no fancy math yet)

Two assets, A and B. Each day they move up or down. Correlation is simply: do they move together or do they take turns being dramatic?

1) “Blocks on rails” correlation intuition

What you should notice
  • When ρ → +1, A and B march together. The “index” (A+B) gets smoother.
  • When ρ → 0, they become independent. The index gets less smooth.
  • When ρ → -1, one zigs while the other zags. The index can become very smooth even if each asset is volatile.
Trader translation

Single names can be violent while the index looks sleepy. That’s dispersion-friendly terrain.

Step 2: Make it quantitative (tiny formulas, big intuition)

Suppose A and B have daily volatilities σA and σB. If you build a simple 50/50 “index”, its variance is:

Basket variance (2 assets)

Var(0.5A + 0.5B) = 0.25σA² + 0.25σB² + 0.5ρσAσB

The only “new” ingredient versus single names is ρ. Correlation is the glue. More glue → more shared movement → index variance changes.

Step 3: What a dispersion trade is (in one picture)

A classic dispersion position (conceptually):

  • Long variance (or options) on A and B (components)
  • Short variance (or options) on the index (A+B basket)

You’re betting that “the hallway sound” (index) is too calm relative to “room sounds” (single names), or that correlation will realize lower than what the index is pricing.

How traders “see” it

The wedge grows when:

  • single-name vols rise
  • correlation falls
  • or the index vol fails to keep up
The correlation jump risk

In a macro panic, correlation can snap higher, and the index can “wake up” fast. Dispersion trades need risk limits.

Step 4: The “3D” view traders love

A neat way to build intuition is to look at index volatility as a surface: as correlation (ρ) changes on one axis, and weight (w) changes on another axis. Even with only two assets, this already explains 80% of real dispersion conversations.

How to read this

When ρ rises, basket σ rises for most weights. When one name dominates (w near 0 or 1), correlation matters less.

From 2 assets to “real” dispersion
  • Index variance depends on all pairwise correlations.
  • “Dispersion” in practice often means: long single-name options, short index options, delta-hedged.
  • When the market is calm but stocks whip around (earnings season, AI narrative shifts), dispersion can thrive.
SEO terms people search

dispersion trading, index vs single stock volatility, correlation trading, variance swap dispersion, implied correlation.

Mini checklist: “Is dispersion on?”

  1. Single names are moving a lot, but the index is stuck in a range.
  2. Event risk is name-specific (earnings, regulation, AI winners/losers).
  3. Index skew is expensive, but single-name skew is not uniformly expensive.
  4. You have a plan for correlation spikes (stop-outs, gamma limits, stress tests).
Takeaway

Correlation is not a philosophy. It’s a dial. Dispersion is the business of getting paid when the dial is priced wrong.
