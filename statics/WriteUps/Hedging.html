In a typical market scenario orders are matched from buy side and sell side. If someone wants to buy 2000 shares at 500/share then we need someone on the opposite side willing to sell 2000 shares at 500/share. Indeed this is a very crude example, coz market making is bit more complex. The above two persons on opposite sides can be a group of people on opposite sides. A trader can place Bid/Offer for a future contract or he/she can place a stoploss order. In a long gamma position it generally results in “buy low sell high” scenario. This can be labeled as earning the spread (offer price -bid price). Conversely in short gamma we place stop loss orders. It takes a bit of practice to understand the spread capturing based on the underlying market dynamics.


One of the crude ways is to place spread orders around daily vol limit. For instance if 1Day vol calculated with 1 Hour Sample data is 10% then spread can be (Spot +/- 1.65*Spot * Vol/100). This intuitively is relying on confidence interval as can easily be seen from the number 1.65. Assuming that return distribution is normal, trader hedges 10% of the time. The vol can be scaled for long duration options. Of course delta hedge required should in integer number of LOTS. The rule is not perfect coz if volatility is low your theta decay is not being hedged properly by gamma.

If the market is moving higher up the huge sell quantity at 110 will influence the market. A trader with a long gamma position can take up a short position at 109 (to play safe!) and a long at 91. A huge sell order might pull the market down, so the rule above has to take into account large orders.

This is again a very specific scenario and not all the up/down movements can be covered by any hedging strategy. Structure of the order book plays a major role in deciding the spread limits. Quants generally calibrate the price movements in a price impact function. This is simply the expected price impact for some Quantity Q of buy/sell order. A simple integration gives the final expected execution price.

So when the gamma is positive a 1 unit change in spot will lead to 1 x (gamma) increase in delta.

The term dS crudely denotes the change in spot. If a trader is expecting upward market trend will he underhedge/overhedge? This is in contrast to a quants world of perfect hedging.

1. Position is Long Delta – Hedge is selling delta → Underhedge (Sell less)

2. Position is Short Delta – Hedge is buying delta → OverHedge (Buy more)


Conversely when gamma is negative 1 unit change in spot will lead to -1 x (gamma) increase in delta or (removing minus sign) a decrease in delta. Intuitively hedging involves predicting the market which is counter intuitive coz we tend to think of hedge as neutralizing market movement. It actually ends up predicting movements and taking position based on short/long term trends.

Volatility hedge for digitals will consist of a set of european options with different maturities (Vega Buckets). The American digital will have Vega sensitivity to the volatilities of these Europeans and we need the model to be calibrated to them in order to show risk against them. As the market moves, the volatility sensitivities can change, for example when the underlying moves closer to the barrier, and thus the model dynamic specified will give different model dependent prices for American digitals.


Most traders do vega bucketing. Hedging long term Vega is also complicated coz it deals with buying/selling illiquid vanillas with long expiries.
