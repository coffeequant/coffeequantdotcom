Heres an Indian puzzle freshly roasted for those caffeine/brainiac geeks. Consider an average girl looking for a suitable match and she interviews around 100 people to select the best match. Once she rejects someone she cant call him back. The suitable mates arrive in random order for instance {good, bad, worst, bad,bad,good....etc}. What strategy she can employ to make sure she maximizes her probability of ending up with best mate.



Of course this is a mathematical puzzle, which to put into clear perspective, lets assume N = Number of candidates, she has lined up (She can stop whenever she wants) R = Number of candidates she decides she has to reject, but will interview them anyway

After rejecting R candidates, the R+1th candidate comes, what is the probability that he is the best one - 1/N When R+2th candidate comes it means she has already rejected R+1th candidate, and so the probability of him being best is still 1/N, but what is the probability of R+1th candidate being worst in the line (past candidates) 1...R+1. It is R/(R+1). This is a subtle conclusion so let me expand....

Lets say R = 3, and first three candidates are Adam (always the first), Animesh (always second), Linus. The ranking order she had till now was that Linus was the best. When 4th candidate comes (R+1), then she decides that R+1th is worst than Linus(best so far), so she has to move on. So probability of best candidate being in first three out of total 4 = 3/4 = 75%, and thus the expression R/(R+1)...and so on for R+2...

So probability of choosing R+2 candidate and him being the best is => 1/N x R/(R+1) Adding up all these probabilities we have,

1/N + 1/N x R/(R+1) + 1/N x (R)/(R+2) + 1/N x (R)/(R+3) or (R/N) [1/R + 1/(R+1) + 1/(R+2) + .......] it can be written as (R/N)[ Summation (i=1 to N (1/i)) - Summation (i=1 to R) (1/i-1)) This reduces to -(R/N) Log(R/N)

The maximum can be found by differentiating this function. The result comes out to be, N/e or 1/e of fraction of candidates should be interviewed and rejected. So R = N/e For N = 100, it comes out to be, approx 36 candidates. This gives probability of 57%. (> 40% passing marks!).
