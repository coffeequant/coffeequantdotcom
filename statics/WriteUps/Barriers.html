Barrier options are very popular in any structured product. There are several mathematical methods for hedging the pin risk at the barrier. Traders prefer to do a barrier shift to accommodate this risk. Now some quantitative finance books have also begun to describe this strategy from a mathematical perspective. I haven't come across books which calculate the amount of barrier shift needed for a particular barrier option. In this article we will discuss how to calculate the barrier shift based on some empirical parameters.

Broadly there are two things which decide the barrier shift

1. Volatility of the Underlying or Basket

2. Liquidity or Volume traded daily for that Underlying

  • In case of baskets we will only consider the volume of the most illiquid underlying

Our example will be a Up and Out Call Option. The parameters will be as follows

Maturity = 1 Year, Spot = 100 and Barrier = 120, and Volatility = 20%, Interest Rate = 1%, Dividend = 0







Since this is a European barrier we have a risk only when the spot is near the barrier at expiry.

Assuming returns are normally distributed with mean 0, the barrier 120 corresponds to 20% return in 1 Year. This is within 1 standard deviation from the mean (sigma * Sqrt(t)). From the z table it can be checked that this probability of P(Z<1) = 1 84% = 16%.

In a day 1 standard deviation movement will correspond to => 1 * sigma * sqrt(1/365) = 1.05% move

Assuming spot was at 119 then we have following High/Low cases

Case 1) 119 * (1+1.05/100) = 120.25

Case 2) 119 * (1 1.05)/100) = 117.76

In case 1 the option value is zero and in case 2 the option value is 17.75. So sensitivity to spot which we call delta at this point might be roughly equal to

Option Value at High(Case2) Option Value at Low(Case1) divided by 1.05 =~ 16.9 (approximately). Why this value is a bit unique? If I take the case of a simple 1 Year ATM Call then delta is around 0.5, so for 100 Calls I buy 50 Futures or generally 1 Lot as a hedge. Here I assume that in this market 1 Lot = 50 Future Contracts. Placing this insanely high delta around this point only for a very short duration can result in loss.

Mathematically if first derivative is high then second derivative might be unstable. Hence Delta being high will suggest gamma is unstable. Let's formalize this by considering an actual contract with a notional of 1 Million USD and Stock price = 100. Assuming option price = 5 USD.

Number of Options Purchaed = 1 million USD / option price = 200,000 Options

For Delta = 0.5, the Number of Futures placed is 0.5 * 200,000 = 100,000 Futures. If 1 Lot = 50 Futures then 2000 Lots Shorted in the market. Traders will generally overhedge or underhedge depending on their market predictions. As the spot moves the delta changes and the hedge is adjusted. We are interesting in knowing the hedge adjustment required 1 month before expiry. The delta change or gamma 1 month before expiry can be easily simulated.

If I had bought the option I would be losing money if the spot hits the barrier, so my hedge will intuitively tell me to buy stock.

1 month simulation before expiry, delta change during 1 day = -0.013

-0.054 * 200,000 = 10,800 Future contracts in a day. Other deltas can similarly be bucketed as follows

With good simulation tools this bucketing can be done each day starting from 1 month remaining to expiry. In this case my Expected Pnl from simulation comes out to be 3200 USD. If my commission on 1 million USD structured product was 1% or 10,000 USD, then I have basically lost 30%.

Barrier Shift

Instead of pricing Barrier = 120, if I price it with 122 then obviously option is cheaper. I still sell it as an option with barrier as 120, so still the payout becomes 0 as soon as 120 is hit. Lets step back and look at the two possible cases

  • Shift Barrier Up or 120 + barriershift

    • Price of option decreases since it has more spot steps to travel before it reaches 0. Compared to 120 barrier option the shifted option is more stable to spot increases. So expected sensitivity to delta is less compared to the original 120 barrier option at same time remaining to expiry.

  • Shift Barrier Down or 120 barriershift

    • Price of option increases since it has less spot steps to travel before it reaches 0. Compared to 120 barrier option the shifted option is less stable to spot increases. So expected sensitivity to delta is more compared to the original 120 barrier option at same time remaining to expiry.

So if a client is willing to sell me the 120 strike option I will quote the price of a shifted option. The amount of shift taken will depend on two things

1. Percentage of Commission lost as a percentage of Hedging near Expiry

2. Number of Contracts as a Percentage of Traded Volume

Some traders also calculate the confidence intervals of mean volume. Quantity of average sized orders placed will help us in calculating mean volume. For example if in a day order size varies between 500 5000 contracts, then mean volume can be safely assumed to be 3000 contracts. As per CLT the confidence interval can easily be calculated for mean volume. Our trading quantity should lie within 1 Standard deviation of the mean volume.

Python code for random walk

import pandas as pd

import numpy as np

barrier_mtgen=np.random.RandomState(5461)

intrate = 0.01

sigma = 0.30

N=365

dt = 1.0/N

sims = 10000

price = np.repeat(0,sims)

for i in arange(sims):

gaussian_variates = barrier_mtgen.normal(np.repeat(0,N))

expfn = exp((intrate-sigma*sigma/2)*dt+sigma*gaussian_variates*(dt**0.5))

expfn = pd.Series(expfn).cumprod()

stock = 100*expfn

stock1 = 101*expfn
