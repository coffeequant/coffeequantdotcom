Dice Puzzle — “Max of k minimums from n dice”

Game: roll n dice at once. Take the minimum in that set. Do that k times. Now keep the maximum of those $k$ minimums.

Sounds innocent. Turns out it’s a great intuition builder for extremes, order stats, “best of the worst,” and why expected value creeps up in layers.

Quick facts

This puzzle came from watching two kids play with dice on a long train ride. The structure is:

repeat

roll n dice → take min → store it

then

after k rounds → take max of those mins

For \(n=6,k=6\), the average answer is around 2.4–2.5. You’re about to reproduce that yourself.

Play it once

Run a lot of trials

Now we repeat the full game many times and build a histogram of the final “max-of-mins.” Watch how the distribution leans upward as you increase n or k.

Tip: bigger n lets you avoid low dice inside a round, and bigger k lets you “shop around” for a lucky round. Both push the final answer higher.

What's going on intuitively?

In each round, the minimum of \(n\) dice is usually low. With 6 dice, someone almost always rolls a 1 or 2, so the min is often ugly.

But you don’t keep that minimum. You repeat the whole round \(k\) times, then you keep the best round — specifically, the highest minimum. One of those rounds will be “surprisingly clean,” where nobody rolled a 1.

That’s why for \(n=6,k=6\), the “final score” (max of mins) averages around 2.4-ish. You’re seeing “the best floor” you managed to produce out of 6 attempts.

Show me the math and let me derive it

1) The minimum of one round

Call the minimum of \(n\) dice \(Y\). For \(Y\) to be at least \(a\), every die in that round has to be \(\ge a\). A single fair die is \(\ge a\) with probability \(\frac{7-a}{6}\) (because values \(a,a+1,\dots,6\)).

Dice are independent, so \[ \mathbb{P}(Y \ge a) = \Big(\tfrac{7-a}{6}\Big)^{n}. \]

That means the chance the minimum is exactly \(a\) is “min is at least \(a\)” minus “min is at least \(a+1\)”:

\[ \mathbb{P}(Y=a) = \Big(\tfrac{7-a}{6}\Big)^{n} - \Big(\tfrac{6-a}{6}\Big)^{n}, \quad a=1,\dots,6. \]

2) The max of \(k\) rounds

Now repeat the whole round \(k\) times. Let \(Y_1,\dots,Y_k\) be the minimum from each round. We keep \[ Z = \max(Y_1,\dots,Y_k). \]

“\(Z \le a\)” means every round had minimum \(\le a\). If \(F_Y(a)=\mathbb{P}(Y \le a)\), then \[ \mathbb{P}(Z \le a) = [F_Y(a)]^{k}. \]

We already know \[ \mathbb{P}(Y \ge a) = \Big(\tfrac{7-a}{6}\Big)^n, \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mathbb{P}(Y \le a) = 1 - \Big(\tfrac{6-a}{6}\Big)^n. \]

So the probability \(Z\) is exactly \(a\) is \[ \mathbb{P}(Z=a) = [F_Y(a)]^{k} - [F_Y(a-1)]^{k}, \] with \(F_Y(a) = 1 - \big(\tfrac{6-a}{6}\big)^n\).

3) Expected score

You can now compute the expected final score:

\[ \mathbb{E}[Z] = \sum_{a=1}^{6} a \cdot \mathbb{P}(Z=a). \]

Or equivalently, use tail probs: \[ \mathbb{E}[Z] = \sum_{a=1}^{6} \mathbb{P}(Z \ge a), \quad \mathbb{P}(Z \ge a) = 1 - [F_Y(a-1)]^{k} = 1 - \Big(1 - \big(\tfrac{7-a}{6}\big)^{n}\Big)^{k}. \]

The point is: the formulas just encode what you already saw in the sim. You’re selecting “the best floor” out of \(k\) tries. Bigger \(n\) (more dice per round) and bigger \(k\) (more chances) both push that floor up.

Rules of the game

1. Roll \(n\) dice at once. Write down the minimum face.

2. Repeat that whole thing \(k\) times → get \(k\) minimums.

3. Your score is the maximum of those \(k\) minimums.

score = max(min(set 1), min(set 2), …, min(set k))

Why it's cool

This is basically an “extreme of an extreme.” First you scrape the floor (the min), then you keep the best floor.

That logic shows up in risk limits, worst-case P&L screens, and even tournament formats (“your worst round doesn’t kill you if you had one really solid round”).

What to look for

Try small \(n\) vs big \(n\). With more dice per round you almost never get a total disaster, so each round’s minimum creeps up.

Then try small \(k\) vs big \(k\). More rounds means more chances for a “clean” round.

Both act like “free optionality.”
