When Everyone Replicates the Put

Equilibrium, instability, and why large-scale put replication changes the drift and volatility you think you’re hedging.

By CoffeeQuant · For structurers, QIS quants, and insurance engineers

1. The basic idea: options don’t live in a vacuum

In textbooks, the underlying price S(t) is exogenous and “lognormal”. You write a put, you delta-hedge it, and the option magically drops out of a clean Black–Scholes world.

In reality, your hedging flow changes the demand for the underlying itself. And when everyone runs the same replication strategy – QIS, portfolio insurance, vol-target indices, structured notes – the usual lognormal story breaks.

A simple way to see that: start from an equilibrium price defined by supply and demand, then inject a systematic hedging flow and see what happens to stability.

2. Excess demand and equilibrium

Let’s define an excess demand function α(S, t, x):

  • S = stock price
  • t = time
  • x = random influences / noise (news, flows, etc.)

At equilibrium, excess demand is zero: 

α(S_eq(t), t, x) = 0

Intuitively:

  • When price goes up, demand should fall (unless you’re in a bubble).
  • When price goes down, demand should rise (unless the company is dying).

So a natural assumption is: 

∂α / ∂S < 0  (downward-sloping demand)

That gives you a stable equilibrium: if price drifts above equilibrium, excess demand turns negative, sellers dominate, and price is pushed back down.

3. Add a trading strategy: delta as extra demand

Now add a systematic trading strategy – e.g. put replication, portfolio insurance, vol-target, or a QIS index that trades the underlying.

Let’s call its net demand Δ(S, t). Then total excess demand is: 

α(S, t, x) + Δ(S, t) = 0   (equilibrium with strategies)

At equilibrium, we have: 

α + Δ = 0

and if price moves a little, the first-order condition is: 

dα + dΔ ≈ 0  ⇒  (∂α/∂S + ∂Δ/∂S) · dS ≈ 0

So the effective slope of demand becomes: 

∂(α + Δ) / ∂S = ∂α/∂S + ∂Δ/∂S

If this slope is negative, the equilibrium remains stable. If the slope turns positive, the equilibrium becomes unstable.

That’s the whole story in one line: when the hedging strategy’s slope is strong enough, it can flip the sign of the total demand curve.

4. A toy functional form for demand

Take a very simple linear form for “fundamental” excess demand: 

α(S) = (1 / β) · (S* − S)
  • S* = “fair” or target price implied by fundamentals
  • β > 0 = how elastic demand is vs price

Then:

∂α / ∂S = −1 / β  < 0  (stable slope)

Now add a strategy demand Δ(S). You can think of Δ as “how many units the hedgers want to buy or sell” as a function of S.

For a delta-hedging strategy of a short put (dealer is short the put), the dealer is typically:

  • short underlying when the put is out of the money,
  • less short as it moves deeper ITM (because gamma hedging makes them buy into sell-offs).

Seen from the market’s perspective, this flow adds an extra term Δ(S). When put replication is done at large scale by many players, the effective ∂Δ/∂S matters.

The new total slope is: 

∂(α + Δ) / ∂S = −1/β + ∂Δ/∂S

If: 

∂Δ/∂S  >  1/β

then ∂(α + Δ)/∂S > 0: the equilibrium becomes locally unstable.

Small price moves now get amplified by hedging flows instead of damped by natural demand.

5. Long gamma, short stability

In the clean Black–Scholes world, a long gamma position is a dream: buy low, sell high, and harvest convexity. The underlying follows: 

dS / S = μ dt + σ dW

with lognormal dynamics and exogenous volatility σ.

But when everyone is implementing a similar long-gamma profile via put replication or portfolio insurance, the underlying is no longer exogenous. Your hedging flows feed back into the drift and volatility of S.

A simple way to write this is:

dS / S = μ_eff(S, t) dt + σ_eff(S, t) dW

where:

  • μ_eff = μ + (impact from aggregate hedging flow / S)
  • σ_eff = σ + (liquidity / impact component)

Once dynamic hedging becomes system-wide, the “lognormal with constant σ” you calibrate to is just a snapshot of this feedback loop, not a structural truth.

6. Simulator: see equilibrium flip from stable to unstable

Below is a toy simulator. It’s deliberately simple:

  • Green: fundamental excess demand α(S) – downward sloping.
  • Orange: demand from put-replication / QIS hedgers Δ(S).
  • Pink: total excess demand α(S) + Δ(S).

As you increase the number of strategies or hedging intensity, the total slope can flip from negative (stable) to positive (unstable).

α(S): fundamental demand Δ(S): strategy demand α(S)+Δ(S): total

Interpretation:

  • When the pink line slopes downward near S*, the market pulls back to equilibrium after a shock.
  • When it slopes upward, small shocks can get amplified: more selling begets more selling.

7. Link to QIS and insurance: put replication inside wrappers

In the insurance world, especially in Asia, a lot of the real gamma sits inside:

  • capital-protected or guaranteed products,
  • IUL / variable UL products with embedded guarantees,
  • QIS-based underlying funds (vol-target, risk-control, trend-following),
  • CPPI-style or constant-proportion rebalancing strategies.

The story is usually told in terms of elegant indices and wrappers. But at the core: you are manufacturing a put, often via dynamic replication on a basket or a vol-target index.

From the perspective of the underlying market, these are just systematic buy/sell rules – i.e. a big Δ(S, t).

Examples:

  • A capital-protected wrapper on a vol-target index: the bank or insurer often replicates a long-dated put through shorter-dated hedges on the index.
  • A “defensive” QIS index that mechanically sells risky assets when volatility spikes: this is a kind of portfolio insurance flow.
  • Structured notes with bespoke capital protection whose underlying is itself a QIS: put-on-put replication layered on top of systematic rebalancing.

When these products scale, your Δ(S, t) is no longer small. It becomes a noticeable component of market microstructure, especially in thin hours or on single-asset underlyings.

8. What happens when everyone uses the same QIS?

The concentration problem is straightforward:

  • Same underlyings (US indices, gold, a popular vol-target index).
  • Same leverage rules (target 5% or 10% vol).
  • Same rebalance triggers (e.g. 3pm fixes, or end-of-day snapshots).

This effectively synchronizes Δ(S, t) across multiple books and issuers.

When volatility rises:

  • Vol-target QIS reduces exposure (selling underlying or futures).
  • Put replication increases short exposure into down-moves (delta hedging).
  • CPPI or risk-control overlays sell risky assets to protect floor values.

On the way down, these systematic sellers can dominate the natural buyers implied by α(S). In our earlier notation: 

∂(α + Δ) / ∂S  >  0  ⇒  local instability

You see this as:

  • air pockets in liquidity,
  • gaps through levels where strikes or triggers cluster,
  • vol spikes that look sharper than “fundamentals” justify.

9. Closing thought

Put replication is elegant on a whiteboard. In production size – inside QIS, insurance wrappers, and structured notes – it becomes a macro flow.

The key shift in mindset is simple:

You’re not pricing an option on a lognormal process.
You’re designing a strategy that helps create the process.

Once you see that, equilibrium diagrams and excess-demand slopes become more than academic toys. They’re a sanity check on whether your next “defensive” idea is actually stabilising the market – or quietly leaning on an unstable equilibrium.
